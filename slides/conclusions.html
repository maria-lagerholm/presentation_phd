This conclusions section summarizes the key outcomes of the doctoral research, which bridged experimental essential oil characterization with machine learning-based structure-activity analysis. The work validated that four commercial EOs meet ISO specifications, demonstrated alginate encapsulation as a viable delivery method, and identified hydroxyl-substituted benzene rings as the strongest predictors of anti-Salmonella activity.

GC-MS Chemotyping: All four oils matched expected chemotypes with >98% identified volatiles. Tea tree was dominated by terpinen-4-ol (45%), lavender by linalyl acetate (42%) with linalool (36%), bergamot by linalyl acetate (58%) with linalool (23%), and peppermint by isomenthol (49%).

Encapsulation: Electrostatic extrusion into calcium-alginate matrices preserved bead integrity post-freeze-drying. Blank beads averaged 2.0mm wet, shrinking ~30% to 1.4mm dry. Oil-loaded beads showed k=0.33-0.40 shrinkage while maintaining sphericity.

Antioxidant: Peppermint is the strongest overall (top on DPPH and ABTS, fresh and after 12 months). All oils declined on storage.

Antimicrobial: Lavender and bergamot inhibited S. Typhimurium at 5 µg/mL; tea tree and peppermint required 10 µg/mL. The linalool/linalyl-acetate chemotype showed superior Gram-negative penetration.

Model Details: L1-regularised logistic regression (C=1.75), 5×100-fold stratified cross-validation. Cross-validated test metrics (Table 4.8): accuracy 0.81, sensitivity 0.83, specificity 0.80, F1 0.82, MCC 0.63, ROC AUC 0.88.

Key Finding: Phenolic -OH on aromatic rings is the strongest predictor (~45% → ~95% activity probability). Carvacrol and thymol (p-cymene phenols) enable protonophoric H⁺ leakage across the outer membrane of S. Typhimurium (Helander et al., 1998; Gören et al., 2004; Peñalver et al., 2005). Eugenol kills via oxidative stress from reactive oxygen species (Aljuwayd et al., 2024).

Positive predictors: Phenolic -OH on aromatic rings, acetate esters, furan heterocycles, cycloalkene motifs.
Negative predictors: Branched bicyclic hydrocarbons, tertiary alcohols, long aliphatic chains.

This explains the order-of-magnitude potency gap between phenolic-rich oregano/thyme oils and the linalyl-acetate-dominated lavender/bergamot examined here.
Limitations

Scope & Constraints

Experimental Phase

  • Only 4 commercial oils — no chemotypic or batch-to-batch coverage
  • Encapsulation: morphology only; no release kinetics, volatile retention, biological activity, or food matrix testing
  • Antioxidants: DPPH & ABTS only; missing FRAP, ORAC, CUPRAC, TEAC
  • Single strain S. Typhimurium ATCC 14028; one culture protocol
  • Other pathogens, clinical isolates & complex food matrices not tested

Computational Phase

  • Heterogeneous MIC protocols (media, inocula, strains)
  • Trace compounds ignored (only constituents >10%)
  • Morgan FPs: no stereochemistry; hash collisions blur attribution
  • Assumes linear additive effects; binary classification only
  • Internal cross-validation only — no external holdout set
  • Small sample (171) vs 682 features risks optimistic metrics
  • Highlighted substructures not mechanistically confirmed in lab
Experimental Limitations: • Only 4 commercial EOs — limited chemotypic and batch variation • Encapsulation: morphology only; no release kinetics, volatile retention, biological activity, or food matrix • Antioxidants: DPPH & ABTS only; missing FRAP, ORAC, CUPRAC, TEAC • Single S. Typhimurium ATCC 14028 strain; one protocol • Other pathogens, clinical isolates and food matrices not tested.
Computational Limitations: • Heterogeneous MIC protocols from literature (media, inocula, strains) • Trace compounds ignored (>10% threshold only) • Morgan FPs lack stereochemistry; hash collisions • Linear additive assumption; binary labels only • Internal CV only, no external holdout • n=171 vs 682 features • Substructure–activity links not mechanistically confirmed in lab.
Future Directions

The Research Horizon

Experimental Expansion

  • Profiling: more oils
  • Antioxidants: more assays
  • Encapsulation: try multiple carriers, do bioactivity tests, test in real foods, etc.
  • Bioactivity: more pathogens, test on biofilms; mechanism of action, multi-omics

Computational & AI

  • Graph neural networks on molecular graphs
  • Transformer molecular language models (stereochemistry-aware)
  • Active-learning loops: predict → wet-lab → retrain
  • ChemBERTa: pretrained on SMILES, further fine-tuned on the small EO dataset
  • AI research agents: autonomously mine literature to expand MIC datasets
Future Directions (from the thesis):
Experimental: Expand beyond four commercial oils across chemotypes, seasons and processing; GC-HRMS, 2D-GC and untargeted LC-MS for trace synergists with reference libraries. Full antioxidant redox panel (FRAP, CUPRAC, ORAC, TEAC) plus liposome ROS / lipid-peroxidation models. Encapsulation: composite carriers (alginate-chitosan, whey-alginate, Pickering), process intensification (multi-nozzle, spinning-disk, continuous freeze-drying), in-line NIR/Raman, and release/stability/aroma in real food matrices. Broader antimicrobial testing: pathogens, clinical isolates, biofilms; vary pH, salt, fat; time-kill, membrane integrity and multi-omics; synergy with conventional preservatives.
Computational & AI: GNNs on molecular graphs; transformer molecular language models; active-learning loops; ChemBERTa fine-tuned on the EO dataset; AI research agents to expand MIC datasets from literature.
