WHO: 1 in 6 Bacterial Infections in 2023 Were Antibiotic-Resistant

The World Health Organization (WHO) has released its Global Antibiotic Resistance Surveillance Report 2025, revealing that one in six bacterial infections worldwide in 2023 were resistant to antibiotics. [Source]

Why studying Salmonella?

S. Typhimurium is a leading global serovar among non-typhoidal infections

Global Health Impact & AMR

  • ~93.8M infections & ~155,000 deaths annually
  • ~510,000 iNTS cases (serious invasive disease)
  • 15–20% of all bacterial foodborne deaths globally
  • MDR S. Typhimurium (DT104): Priority Pathogen
  • Resistant to ampicicillin, chloramphenicol, sulfonamides

Epidemiology & Persistence

  • Reservoirs: >90 species (poultry, cattle, pigs, rodents)
  • CFR (iNTS): 10–25% (highest in sub-Saharan Africa)
  • Risk Groups: Children <5, HIV+ adults, malaria areas
  • Persistence: Months/years on dry foods; weeks on surfaces
  • Growth: 7–45 °C (optimal ~37 °C)
Sources: WHO NTS, Frontiers (2025), PLOS NTDs, WHO AMR
Antibiotics are routinely added to animal feed and water on farms, both to promote growth and prevent disease in livestock. This agricultural use creates an environment where bacteria including Salmonella Typhimurium is exposed to low antibiotic levels, encouraging the bacteria to adapt and develop resistance. As a result, resistant strains emerge in animals before entering the food chain and causing hard-to-treat infections in people. While many Salmonella infections are treated without antibiotics, multidrug resistance is a clinical crisis because effective antibiotics are the only thing standing between life and death for invasive cases. Today, it's a top WHO priority because our treatments are failing.
